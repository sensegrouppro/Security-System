## Why Commercial CCTV Installation Is Different From Residential


This is worth saying upfront. Commercial and residential installations are two different things entirely.

A home setup might involve four wireless cameras plugged into a cloud account. That works fine for monitoring a driveway or front door. A commercial property is a different situation.

Commercial buildings have more square footage, more entry points, more people moving through them, and higher stakes for theft and liability. The equipment needs to run continuously, 24 hours a day, 7 days a week, in conditions ranging from -30°C Ontario winters to summer humidity. Footage needs to hold up in insurance claims and police investigations. Systems need to integrate with access control, alarm systems, and business networks.

That's why [commercial CCTV installation](https://sense-group.pro/security-cameras-installation/toronto/) is a structured, multi-stage process rather than a quick afternoon job. Let's go through each step.


## Step 1: Security Assessment and Site Survey


Every professional commercial CCTV installation starts here. No reputable installer should quote a system without walking your property first.

At [Sense Group](https://sense-group.pro/), we offer free onsite assessments for all Toronto and GTA businesses. Here is what that visit covers:

**Mapping entry and exit points.** Every door, gate, loading dock, emergency exit, and vehicle access point gets noted. These are always priority camera locations.

**Identifying high-risk zones.** Cash handling areas, server rooms, inventory storage, and anywhere valuable assets are kept or moved.

**Evaluating existing infrastructure.** We check whether your building already has structured cabling in place. This affects both installation time and cost significantly.

**Assessing lighting conditions.** We look at which areas have adequate lighting and which rely on cameras with night vision. This matters a lot for Toronto properties with outdoor parking lots or rear loading areas that sit in darkness for a large chunk of the year.

**Checking for obstructions.** Shelving units, signage, support pillars, and HVAC equipment all affect sightlines. Camera placement needs to account for these before a single hole is drilled.

**Evaluating network setup.** Modern IP camera systems connect to your business network. We assess your existing infrastructure to plan how cameras will connect to the NVR and how remote access will be configured.

The site assessment typically takes 30 to 90 minutes depending on property size. After the visit, we have a clear, accurate picture of what your system needs.


## Step 2: System Design and Camera Placement Planning

With the site assessment done, we design the system. This is where the technical planning happens before any physical work begins.

**Camera count and placement.** Based on the assessment, we determine exactly how many cameras are needed and where each one mounts. We position cameras to eliminate blind spots while avoiding redundant coverage that adds cost without adding value.

**Camera height.** Commercial cameras perform best when mounted at 2.5 to 3 metres (roughly 8 to 10 feet). High enough to prevent tampering, low enough to capture clear facial detail. For large open spaces like warehouses, ceiling-mounted wide-angle cameras work better.

**Camera type selection per location.** Dome cameras work well indoors and in areas where a low-profile appearance matters. Bullet cameras are better for long-range outdoor coverage like parking lots and perimeter fencing. PTZ cameras are used where active monitoring or wide-area coverage is needed.

**Cable routing plan.** We map out how Cat6 cables will run from each camera location back to the NVR. Clean, concealed cable management protects wires from damage and keeps your property looking professional.

**NVR placement.** The NVR (Network Video Recorder) goes in a secure, ventilated location, usually a server room, IT closet, or locked cabinet. It should be accessible for maintenance but not visible to general staff or visitors.

**Storage calculation.** We calculate the storage capacity you need based on camera count, video resolution, frames per second, and your required retention period (typically 14 to 30 days for most Toronto commercial properties).

This planning stage is where professional installations differ most from rushed, low-cost jobs. A poorly planned system leads to blind spots, cable runs that get damaged, and equipment that underperforms. Getting the design right first saves time and money on the installation itself.

## Step 3: Equipment Selection


Once the system design is approved, we confirm the equipment list. Here are the core components of a commercial CCTV system:

### IP Security Cameras

We work with leading commercial brands including Axis, Ubiquiti, Verkada, UNV, and Hikvision. For most Toronto commercial installations, our standard cameras are 4MP network cameras offering:

- 2560 x 1440 resolution at 25 fps
- 2.8mm fixed lens with 137° angle of view
- Smart IR night vision up to 30 metres
- Starlight technology for ultra low-light performance
- Digital noise reduction
- Built-in microphone
- IP67 weather resistance rating
- Operating range from -30°C to 60°C

That last point is not an accident. Canadian winters are hard on outdoor equipment, and cameras rated for -30°C are a necessity for any property across the GTA, Barrie, Hamilton, or anywhere else in Ontario.

### Network Video Recorder (NVR)

The NVR stores and manages all footage from your IP cameras. We use 4K NVR units with enough channels to cover your camera count and expandability for future additions. Key specs to confirm include channel count, maximum storage capacity, and remote access compatibility.

### Network Cabling

We use Cat6 Ethernet cable for all camera connections. Cat6 supports the bandwidth requirements of high-resolution IP cameras and provides a reliable, interference-resistant signal over long runs. In some installations, Cat7 is the better choice — we advise on this based on your specific setup.

### PoE Switch or PoE Injectors

Power over Ethernet (PoE) lets a single Cat6 cable deliver both data and power to each camera. This eliminates the need for separate power outlets at each camera location and simplifies the installation considerably. For larger systems, a PoE network switch manages power and connectivity from a central point.

### Mounting Hardware

Camera mounting brackets, junction boxes, conduit, weatherproof housings for outdoor cameras, and tamper-resistant hardware. The right mounting hardware matters for longevity, especially on Toronto exteriors that face freeze-thaw cycles every winter.

### Uninterruptible Power Supply (UPS)

A UPS keeps your NVR and network switch running during a power outage. If your cameras go offline the moment the power flickers, your system offers no coverage during exactly the kind of disruption that creates security vulnerabilities. A UPS is a straightforward investment that pays for itself.

## Step 4: Network Cabling Installation

With the design confirmed and equipment ordered, physical installation begins with the cabling. This is often the most labour-intensive part of a commercial CCTV installation.

**Cable routing.** Our technicians run Cat6 cables from each planned camera location back to the NVR location. In commercial buildings, this usually means running cables through ceiling tiles, conduit, cable trays, or behind wall finishes.

**Concealed installation.** We keep cabling as concealed and organized as possible. Exposed cables on walls or ceilings are not just an eyesore — they're a vulnerability. A cable that can be seen can be cut or damaged. Our technicians design cable routes that minimize visibility without sacrificing accessibility for future maintenance.

**Cable labelling.** Every cable gets labelled at both ends before termination. This sounds like a small detail, but it matters enormously when you need to troubleshoot a camera three years down the road. Proper labelling means any technician can trace any cable in minutes rather than hours.

**Conduit on exterior runs.** Any cables that must run on building exteriors go inside weatherproof conduit. This protects against UV degradation, physical damage, and water ingress — all very real concerns on Canadian commercial properties.

**RJ45 termination and testing.** Every cable gets terminated with RJ45 connectors and tested with a cable tester before any camera goes up. A faulty cable run discovered before installation is a five-minute fix. One discovered after a camera is mounted and configured is a much bigger job.

## Step 5: Camera Mounting

With cabling in place and tested, cameras go up. Mounting looks straightforward but has real consequences for coverage quality.

**Height and angle.** We mount cameras at the pre-planned height and angle from the design stage. For facial recognition purposes, cameras angled slightly downward at 2.5 to 3 metres capture the best usable detail. For parking lot coverage, we position bullet cameras at higher points with the angle adjusted for distance.

**Bracket installation.** Mounting brackets go into solid surfaces — concrete, metal framing, wood blocking. A camera that wobbles or shifts in the wind is a camera with constantly changing coverage angles and useless time-lapse footage.

**Weatherproofing exterior mounts.** All exterior camera installations get weatherproof junction boxes and sealed cable entry points. Water ingress through a cable entry point on an outdoor camera is a common cause of premature camera failure. We seal every exterior penetration properly.

**Cable connections.** Each camera connects to its pre-run Cat6 cable. Connections get checked for secure termination and correct polarity before powering up.

**Initial positioning check.** Before moving to the NVR configuration, we power each camera individually and verify the live image on a monitor or tablet. We check the framing, look for obstructions we might have missed, and confirm the coverage angle matches the plan.

## Step 6: NVR Setup and Configuration

With cameras mounted and connected, the NVR setup begins. This is where the system starts to come alive.

**NVR mounting and connection.** The NVR gets installed in its pre-planned secure location — server room, IT closet, or locked cabinet — and connected to the network switch and cameras.

**Hard drive installation and formatting.** Storage drives get installed and formatted. For most commercial properties, we recommend enough storage for at least 30 days of continuous recording at full resolution. High-security environments may need 60 or 90 days.

**Camera discovery and registration.** The NVR scans the network and discovers all connected cameras. Each camera gets added to the system, assigned a channel, and given a descriptive label (e.g., "Front Entrance," "Loading Dock," "Server Room").

**Recording schedule configuration.** We configure recording schedules based on your specific needs. Most commercial clients set continuous 24/7 recording. Some businesses prefer motion-triggered recording during off-hours to preserve storage capacity. We advise on the best approach for your operation.

**Resolution and frame rate settings.** We configure each camera to record at full 4MP resolution and an appropriate frame rate. Higher frame rates capture smoother motion but consume more storage. For most commercial applications, 15 fps is the practical standard. Entry points and cash handling areas typically record at higher rates.

**Motion detection zones.** We draw motion detection zones on each camera feed — active areas that trigger alerts when movement is detected. A camera covering a parking lot doesn't need to trigger alerts every time a car drives past on the street. Proper zone configuration cuts false alerts dramatically.

**Alert configuration.** Push notifications can be sent to your phone or email when specific cameras detect motion during defined time windows. After-hours alerts for restricted areas are especially useful for retail and warehouse clients.

**Password and network security.** Every NVR and camera gets strong, unique passwords. We also configure the system on a dedicated VLAN (Virtual Local Area Network) where possible, isolating camera traffic from your main business network. This is standard cybersecurity practice for commercial surveillance systems.


## Step 7: Remote Access Setup

Remote access is one of the most practical features of a modern IP camera system. We configure this during installation so you can view live and recorded footage from your phone, tablet, or laptop — from anywhere.

**Mobile app setup.** We install and configure the manufacturer's mobile app (or a compatible third-party app) on your devices. We walk you through logging in, switching between cameras, and pulling up recorded footage.

**Port forwarding and network configuration.** For remote access to work reliably from outside your network, the NVR needs to be accessible through your router. We configure this securely, using manufacturer-recommended ports and access controls.

**User account setup.** We set up user accounts with appropriate access levels. You might want your security manager to have full admin access, while other staff can only view live feeds without the ability to delete footage or change settings.

**Remote access testing.** Before we leave, we test remote access from outside your network — using a cellular connection rather than your building's Wi-Fi — to confirm it works as expected from a real-world scenario.


## Step 8: Full System Testing

Every camera. Every cable. Every recording function. Nothing gets signed off without passing a full test.

Here is what our testing covers:

**Live image quality check.** We review each camera feed for clarity, correct framing, adequate brightness, and absence of obstructions. If a camera needs angle adjustment, we do it now.

**Night vision test.** Where possible, we test each camera's infrared and low-light performance. For outdoor cameras, this means checking IR illumination reach and confirming there's no IR reflection from nearby surfaces that would wash out the image.

**Recording verification.** We confirm that every camera is recording to the NVR, that footage timestamps are accurate, and that the storage system is writing files correctly.

**Motion detection test.** We walk through each camera's detection zone to confirm motion alerts are firing as configured. We adjust sensitivity settings where needed to reduce false triggers.

**Remote access confirmation.** Final remote access check from outside the building network.

**Playback test.** We pull up recorded footage from each camera and scrub through a few minutes to confirm playback quality and timeline accuracy.

Any issue found during testing gets fixed before the job is considered complete. This step is non-negotiable.

## Step 10: Ongoing Maintenance

A commercial CCTV system needs periodic maintenance to perform reliably over its lifespan.

**Lens cleaning.** Outdoor cameras accumulate dust, bird droppings, and condensation on lenses. Dirty lenses reduce image quality significantly. We recommend cleaning exterior camera lenses every three to six months.

**Firmware updates.** Camera and NVR manufacturers release firmware updates that fix bugs, close security vulnerabilities, and improve performance. Updates should be applied at least once or twice a year.

**Hard drive health checks.** NVR hard drives have a finite lifespan. Monitoring drive health and replacing drives proactively avoids unexpected failures — and the lost footage that comes with them.

**Coverage review.** If your property layout changes — new shelving, a renovated floor, an expanded parking area — camera placement should be reviewed to confirm coverage hasn't been compromised.

**System audit after incidents.** If a security incident occurs, a post-incident audit is a good practice. Review not just the relevant footage but the performance of the whole system. Were there gaps? Did all cameras record cleanly? Use incidents as an opportunity to improve, not just document.

## Ontario Privacy Considerations for Commercial CCTV

This section matters. Installing security cameras in a commercial property in Ontario is legal and common practice, but there are guidelines that responsible businesses should follow.

**PIPEDA compliance.** The Personal Information Protection and Electronic Documents Act (PIPEDA) governs how businesses collect and handle personal information, which includes video footage. Commercial CCTV is generally permitted under PIPEDA when used for legitimate security purposes, but you should limit recording to areas where there is a genuine security need. Staff washrooms, change rooms, and areas with a reasonable expectation of privacy should not be covered by cameras.

**Signage.** It is standard practice to post visible signage indicating that CCTV is in operation on the premises. This informs employees, customers, and visitors that surveillance is active and is considered best practice for commercial properties in Ontario.

**Footage access and retention.** Footage should only be accessed by authorized personnel. Most commercial businesses retain footage for 30 days, after which it is overwritten. If footage is required for a police matter or insurance claim, it should be exported and secured separately.

**Employee notification.** If cameras monitor employee work areas, staff should be informed that surveillance is in place. This is both a legal best practice and a practical one — it removes ambiguity and reduces the chance of disputes later.

If you have specific questions about privacy compliance for your business, speaking with an Ontario lawyer familiar with PIPEDA requirements is the right step. We are security camera installers, not legal counsel, and the regulations can vary by situation.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Do I need a permit to install commercial security cameras in Toronto?**
In most cases, no. Standard commercial camera installations in Toronto do not require a building permit. If the installation involves significant structural modifications, wiring through fire-rated assemblies, or changes to a heritage building, you should check with your building management or the City of Toronto. Our team advises on this during the site assessment.

**Can I install cameras in employee work areas?**
Yes, in most commercial settings. Under PIPEDA, monitoring employee work areas for legitimate security or operational purposes is generally permitted. Employees should be informed that surveillance is in place. Cameras in washrooms, change rooms, or areas with a reasonable expectation of privacy are not permitted.

**What is the difference between an NVR and a DVR?**
An NVR (Network Video Recorder) works with IP cameras connected over an Ethernet network. A DVR (Digital Video Recorder) works with older analog cameras connected over coaxial cable. For new commercial installations, we use NVR-based systems. They deliver better image quality, remote access capability, and scalability that DVR systems cannot match.

**How much storage does a commercial CCTV system need?**
This depends on camera count, resolution, frame rate, and retention period. A 4-camera system recording at 4MP with 30-day retention at 15 fps requires roughly 1 to 2TB of storage. An 8-camera system at the same settings needs 3 to 4TB. We calculate the right storage capacity during the system design stage so you never run short.

**What happens to footage when the storage is full?**
NVRs are configured to overwrite the oldest footage when storage capacity is reached. This happens automatically. If you need footage retained beyond the standard overwrite cycle — for an ongoing investigation or insurance claim — we can export it to a USB drive or configure cloud backup.

**Can cameras be integrated with an alarm system?**
Yes. Modern commercial CCTV systems can integrate with alarm systems so that camera motion detection triggers alarm alerts, or an alarm event automatically flags the corresponding camera footage. This creates a more complete security response. We handle integration during installation.

**What brands of security cameras do you install?**
We work with Axis, Ubiquiti, Verkada, UNV, and Hikvision. Each brand has strengths in specific commercial environments. We recommend the best fit for your property type, performance requirements, and budget during the assessment.

**My building has concrete walls. Does that make installation harder?**
It does add time and labour cost. Running cables through concrete requires core drilling, which takes longer than routing through drywall or ceiling tiles. We account for this in our site assessment and quote. It's a common situation in older Toronto commercial buildings and industrial properties, and our team handles it regularly.
